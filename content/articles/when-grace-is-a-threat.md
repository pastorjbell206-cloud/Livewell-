# When Grace is a Threat: The Disciples, Saul, and the Walls We Build

In Acts 9, we read one of the most remarkable transition moments in the early church. Saul of Tarsus, the man who had ravaged the church, shows up in Jerusalem claiming to be a disciple. The response of the believers there is completely understandable, and yet profoundly challenging to how we think about grace today.

> "And when he had come to Jerusalem, he attempted to join the disciples. And they were all afraid of him, for they did not believe that he was a disciple." (Acts 9:26)

They were afraid of him. They didn't believe him. They wanted to build a wall to keep him out.

## The Instinct to Protect

The disciples in Jerusalem weren't being faithless; they were being careful. They had seen the damage Saul had done. They knew the threat he posed. But here is the hard truth we often miss: *careful* is often just a spiritualized word for *fearful*.

We want grace to be safe. We want it to operate within our categories and on our timelines. When God saves the person we have built our identity around opposing, it feels like a threat to our safety. If your theology has no room for your enemy’s redemption, your theology is too small for the cross.

We see this same instinct today. In an era of intense tribal sorting, we prefer our enemies clearly marked. We build theological, political, and cultural walls to protect our purity. But the gospel doesn't just change the monster; it demands that the victims learn to sit at the table with him. That is the terrifying, beautiful risk of grace.

## The Barnabas Intervention

The story would have ended there—with a walled-off church and a rejected apostle—if not for one man.

> "But Barnabas took him and brought him to the apostles and declared to them how on the road he had seen the Lord..." (Acts 9:27)

Every converted person needs a Barnabas. Someone has to spend their own credibility on the new believer. Barnabas didn't just introduce Saul; he sponsored him. He took a risk. He extended credibility to someone who had not yet earned it in Jerusalem.

The church only gets the Apostle Paul because one man was willing to be wrong about Saul.

The church in America today is starving for people willing to risk their reputation on the transforming power of the gospel. We are often too busy curating our own purity to sponsor anyone else's progress. We want the fruit of transformation without the risk of association.

## The Peace That Follows the Risk

What happens when we take that risk? What happens when we welcome the threat into the family?

> "So the church throughout all Judea and Galilee and Samaria had peace and was being built up." (Acts 9:31)

Peace didn't come because they built better walls. Peace came because they welcomed the threat into the family. When we stop trying to protect the church from the world, and start trusting the Spirit to transform the world, the church is built up.

Because Christ stepped across the ultimate line—from heaven to a cross—to sponsor you when you were still an enemy, you can afford to be a Barnabas today.

Who is the person you have decided is beyond the reach of grace? What would it look like to extend them a little credibility? Who needs your sponsorship this week?
